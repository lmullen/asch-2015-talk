The question is whether we can extend the kind of work that we are all doing in cities to the United States more generally?

Gaustad map

The maps in the Gaustad atlas are by and large dependent on the questions asked in the U.S. Census.

I don't want to criticize them overly, but there are two complaints. First that they are focused on comparison across denominations. But second they have a problem with trying to make everything local to the time and place.

This census data on religion is mostly available in the National Historical GIS. But far more information is available in the census records.

The reliability of the censuses of religion has been defended in Rodney Stark, “The Reliability of Historical United States Census Data on Religion,” Sociology of Religion 53, no. 1 (1992): 91–95, doi:10.2307/3711631.

Some of this is tracking down more information than simply number of churches. (Number of churches, by the way, is a poor measure of comparison, since Catholics and Jews for example had very large congregations, while other denominations had very small congregations.) The Census records contain far more information about different denominations. I can't look at the volumes without learning about some religious group I did not know about before. But there is also more geographic specificity. Each of the censuses contains lists of members and organizations in cities with more than 25,000 people. None of us is likely to do a project on Council Bluffs, Iowa, or Woonsocket, RI, but it is possible to know more than the Gaustad maps come close to telling us. For example, there are 292 cities of that description in the 1926 census containing records by my count of 215 separate denominations.

United States Department of Commerce, Bureau of the Census, Religious Bodies: 1926 (Washington: Government Printing Office, 1930), 1:348–59; 2:iii-vii.

This is not big data. Computationally speaking, this is not much data at all: it's just a couple steps above trivial. Historically speaking, though, it is daunting. That means we are in a good place, where we are doing the work of historians (digital historians).

Big data, for my money, can be defined as data which cannot fit into RAM, or data where the size of the data approaches the size of the population. This is neither.
