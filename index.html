On my desk I keep one reference work within easy reach: the New Historical Atlas of Religion in America. It is not just that I am fascinated by maps. When writing or teaching about any religious group, I try to remember the dictum that I first learned from Jane Kamensky: “events take place.” To bring place into my work, I need to remind myself constantly of the where and when of American religious history.

Edwin S. Gaustad, Philip L. Barlow, and Richard W. Dishno, New Historical Atlas of Religion in America (New York: Oxford University Press, 2001).

But I also keep in mind Laurie Maffly-Kipps’s memorably titled essay “If It’s South Dakota You Must Be Episcopalian.” In that essay Maffly-Kipp reviews several atlases of American religion and makes a number of telling critiques of the whole enterprise of mapping religion, and indeed mapping in the humanities generally. Most of these critiques can be lumped into two categories: sources and definitions. First, sources: “We are still limited by data that is partial, ambiguous, and clearly slanted toward things that can be counted and people that traditionally have been seen as significant.” In other words, our sources for making maps of religion aren’t very good, and the best sources let us make maps of groups that we already know a lot about. Second, definitions: Maffly-Kipp observes that our “our dazzling new technologies and spatial theories” might only have “brought us back to much more circumscribed definitions of religious experience.” In other words, map-making requires us to create categories, almost always denominational categories, when the whole trend of religious history and religious studies is towards more complex, layered understandings of religion.

Laurie F. Maffly-Kipp, “If It’s South Dakota You Must Be Episcopalian: Lies, Truth-Telling, and the Mapping of U.S. Religion,” Church History 71, no. 1 (2002): 132–42. Cf. Laurie F. Maffly-Kipp, “Putting Religion on the Map,” The Journal of American History 94, no. 2 (2007): 522–29, doi:10.2307/25094964.

The scholars who have created the leading atlases of American religion were not unaware of the theoretical questions raised by Maffly-Kipp, and often raised them in their prefaces. Nor should we be overly critical of the form of the New Historical Atlas or of Bret Carrol's Routledge Historical Atlas of Religion in America. When I look at these atlases, I see how they are straining the seams of possibility when it comes to mapping in print. So my question is this. It seems to me that digital mapmaking means that we have new possibilities for rethinking the forms and escaping the constraints of mapmaking in print atlases (let alone in monographic scholarship). But can digital mapmaking let us make maps which address the theoretical constraints raised by both Maffly-Kipp and the mapmakers? How do we get from the work of Gaustad et al. to the next step, while taking into account Maffly-Kipp's concerns?

Bret E. Carroll, The Routledge Historical Atlas of Religion in America (New York: Routledge, 2000).

So within that framework, my aim is to show you two projects. The first is a class I taught on mapping Boston's religions. And, since I've done more interesting work since I submitted my abstract a year ago, I'm also going to sketch out the sources and methods for digital maps of religion on a much larger scale.

My general argument is that there are large sources of data which cartographers of religion have not used. They have not used them because they are unaware of them: I dare say that the scholars who created the atlases know the sources better than I currently do. Rather the problem is one of resolution. Those sources weren't adapted because they could never be meaningfully depicted in print atlases. The great difficulty with this work is comparison, which forces you into a least common-denominator approach, especially if you set your chronological scope so big so that you're dealing with radically different sources. Digital maps have the potential to improve some, but not all, of these questions. Our maps of religion so far have dealt with only a tiny fraction of the information available to us. So I suggest that a deeper map tells us more, and more humanistic, things about religious history.

Second, digital maps offer us many techniques for mapping. But I do think that digital maps offer the possibility for better use of scale and change over time. While neither of these entirely deals with the problem of mapping humanistically, they do permit us to at least start to address these theoretical concerns. I'm going to present this through a deep map of religion in Boston, and then through a few extensive maps of Congregationalists.

Gaustad map

The maps in the Gaustad atlas are by and large dependent on the questions asked in the U.S. Census.

I don't want to criticize them overly, but there are two complaints. First that they are focused on comparison across denominations. But second they have a problem with trying to make everything local to the time and place.

This census data on religion is mostly available in the National Historical GIS. But far more information is available in the census records.

The reliability of the censuses of religion has been defended in Rodney Stark, “The Reliability of Historical United States Census Data on Religion,” Sociology of Religion 53, no. 1 (1992): 91–95, doi:10.2307/3711631.

Some of this is tracking down more information than simply number of churches. (Number of churches, by the way, is a poor measure of comparison, since Catholics and Jews for example had very large congregations, while other denominations had very small congregations.) The Census records contain far more information about different denominations. I can't look at the volumes without learning about some religious group I did not know about before. But there is also more geographic specificity. Each of the censuses contains lists of members and organizations in cities with more than 25,000 people. None of us is likely to do a project on Council Bluffs, Iowa, or Woonsocket, RI, but it is possible to know more than the Gaustad maps come close to telling us. For example, there are 292 cities of that description in the 1926 census containing records by my count of 215 separate denominations.

United States Department of Commerce, Bureau of the Census, Religious Bodies: 1926 (Washington: Government Printing Office, 1930), 1:348–59; 2:iii-vii.

Chart comparing Gaustad's bandwidth and mine.

This is not big data. Computationally speaking, this is not much data at all: it's just a couple steps above trivial. Historically speaking, though, it is daunting. That means we are in a good place, where we are doing the work of historians (digital historians).

Big data, for my money, can be defined as data which cannot fit into RAM, or data where the size of the data approaches the size of the population. This is neither.
